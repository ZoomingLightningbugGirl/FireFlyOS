    • Design Process

    1. Ask:

      What are some of the constraints of this project? What are the success criteria? For me, this essentially meant expressing my interest in computer science in an interactive format. Some of the constraints were that this must be made with markup languages and JavaScript. I have a thorough knowledge about HTML and CSS, but I only began learning JavaScript recently through my highschool computer science course. So, I often needed to reference documentation for extra interactive elements of my website.

    2. Imagine:

      How can one compress their passions and achievements into one streamlined source? The answer: An operating system! I decided to make one with my previously acquired markup language skills, and it could help me build on my very fundamental JavaScript knowledge. Also, I would get to have something to show for my skills directly.

    3. Plan:

      How will you convey information to the user effectively? If there's one thing I've learned from my computer science class, it's that you can't just "wing it" when it comes to a website. It needs research and thoughtful graphic design, especially since the organization of a website can be the difference between a user's continued use or abandonment. I decided to use an F-layout in my website and use Adobe Colors to build a color palette. I also used PhotoPea, a cheaper option to PhotoShop, so I could make a grid layout of my landing page and organization.

    4. Create:

      How will you turn your ideas into reality? For me, I chose to deconstruct the objectives for each page into small chunks to make it less daunting.

    Ever since I was young, I've loved being a creator. When I set out to do something and it ends up working, it is just a magical feeling. Through the process, despite your flaws, you can't help feeling just a little bit like a god of your own world. I fell in love with that freedom, with that tool of making. This inspired me to learn Python and markup languages, then become a programmer. I was obsessed with game development for a long time, as I was an excited pre-teen ready to make her mark on the world. I made a few games with Scratch, then Unreal Engine 5, then finally Godot; but game development burnt me out a bit— I must admit. Then, the rise of AI began to take jobs in game development and software development. I discovered cybersecurity. I learned that I'm not only able to produce code, but trick it into my bidding. This started my journey as an ethical hacker, and I wish to continue this journey and get better at it. I anticipate learning PHP, BASH, and MySQL to better work in hacking.
    I loved video game design, I truly did, but it was very difficult. I loved the thrill of seeing it all come together, but the hours and hours of frustrated debugging in a AAA game engine (cough, UE5...) then being unable to package my game as an HTML5 iframe burnt me out. I decided to take a bit of a break from it, and then started learning more about cybersecurity from HackTheBox. I realized that not only was this more practical, but it was also very fun and dynamic due to each site being different. It felt like a puzzle I was trying to solve, just like the ones from my childhood. Someday, I hope to be able to try CTFs and Bug Bounty Hunting.
